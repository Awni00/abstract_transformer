Thank you for your review, including the positive comments about the work and the contributions that it makes. Your questions and detailed comments are very perceptive, and we aim to provide detailed responses below.

**Summary of responses**
- We expand the discussion of related work.
- We comment on how positional embeddings can indeed be viewed as symbol tokens.
- We provide further detail on how the nonlinear MLP mappings process both relational and sensory information.

> While there is a comparison with RCA, which is closely related work, there seems to be a lack of information on existing related works or literature. 

There exists a line of work exploring relational architectures and inductive biases, including RelNet (Santoro et al., 2017), PrediNet (Shanahan et al. 2020), ESBN (Webb et al., 2021), CoRelNet (Kerg et al., 2022), and the Abstractor (Altabaa et al., 2024). The latter is the most closely related to our work. For the most part, these architectures have been relatively narrow in their domain of applicability, being evaluated primarily on synthetic relational tasks like the relational games benchmark that we consider in Section 4.1. Nonetheless, the computational mechanisms proposed in this line of work shows real promise in relational processing and reasoning. By contrast, Transformers are versatile general-purpose sequence models that can be applied to a wide range of task paradigms and data modalities. The goal of our work is to design and incorporate relational computational mechanisms into the Transformer framework, yielding an architectural framework with enhanced relational processing capabilities without sacrificing general sequence processing in other areas.

Interestingly, some studies have shown how Transformers can apparently *implicitly* learn abstractions and relations when they are trained on sufficiently large amounts of data. In particular, Webb, Holyoak and Lu ("Emergent analogical reasoning in large language models", Nat. Human Behav., 2023) show how LLMs (GPT-3) show relational reasoning on versions of Ravens matrix type problems, even when not directly trained on such tasks. Other evidence of this implicit relational reasoning ability is discussed in work such as Dasgupta et al. (2024), Srivastava et al. (2023), Wei et al. (2022), and Chan et al. (2024) and we will add a discussion of this line of work to the revised paper. One implication of our study, however, is that by directly incorporating a relational attention mechanism as we do in *DAT*, it is possible to perform relational tasks with much greater sample efficiency, while also improving on tasks that benefit from combined sensory and relational information processing, such as language modeling and image classification.

> In Transformers, they also use symbolic tokens (e.g., position embedding). Might it be more precise to add the symbol vector to the context tokens on the right-hand side of Figure 1 (a)?


This is a good point. There exists several ways of incorporating positional encoding in Transformers, and one way is to add positional embeddings at the zero-th layer before self-attention (though typically they are only added once at the beginning, whereas we add symbols at every relational attention operation in the model).

However, the key aspect of relational attention in this work is that the "values" that are transmitted in relational attention do not contain sensory information; the values are relational, and the positional encoding symbols are only used to identify which objects the relations pertain to. We will expand on this point and mention the reviewer's good point about standard positional encoding viewed as symbols in the discussion.

> In the Dual Attention process, sensory and relational embeddings are concatenated. I wonder how they are pooled before entering the next layer. 

This is another good question. One can imagine different types of pooling operations. We have chosen to concatenate them, and then they are processed together by the MLP that is part of each layer. Intuitively, the MLP can learn to select from and combine the sensory or relational information as needed. In future work it will be interesting to explore alternative ways of combining (pooling) the different sources of information.
